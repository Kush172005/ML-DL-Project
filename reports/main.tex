      We address multi-step hourly energy forecasting on the ETTh1 benchmark
      (17,420 observations, target oil temperature, horizon $H=24$) using a
      four-stage hybrid pipeline.
      SARIMAX captures linear seasonality; a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)
      detects volatility regimes from residuals; \textbf{TemporalHybridNet}---a
      bidirectional LSTM with multi-head self-attention and a gated residual
      connection---predicts 24-step residuals; the final forecast is the additive
      sum.
      Phase~2 introduces cyclical time features, AdamW with warm-up and cosine
      decay, weight initialisation strategies, and a full ablation study.
      All results are reported against a seasonal naive (lag-24) trivial baseline
      and accompanied by training curves, per-horizon diagnostics, and ablation
      plots.

\section{Introduction}
% ===========================================================

High-frequency energy and load series exhibit a superposition of two distinct
phenomena: \emph{predictable structure} (hourly and weekly seasonality) and
\emph{nonlinear residuals} driven by weather events, equipment behaviour, and
demand shocks.
Purely statistical models such as SARIMAX excel at the former but cannot
model the latter; purely neural models risk overfitting when the seasonal
signal dominates.

We address this tension via \textbf{residual decomposition}: SARIMAX explains
the linear seasonal component; a compact deep model learns what remains; a
regime-aware Gaussian mixture provides context.
The architecture is designed to be \emph{modular}---each block is independently
interpretable and evaluable.

\paragraph{Task.}
Forecast \textbf{OT} (oil temperature, ETTh1) for $H=24$ future hours using
covariates HUFL, HULL, MUFL, MULL, and cyclical calendar features.
Chronological splits (60\,/\,20\,/\,20) ensure no temporal leakage.

% ===========================================================
\section{Related Work}
% ===========================================================

\textbf{Classical baselines.}
ARIMA, SARIMAX, and exponential smoothing (ETS) provide strong, interpretable
baselines for seasonal series~\citep{hyndman2021forecasting}.
They are linear, however, and cannot adapt to residual structure.

\textbf{Deep sequence models.}
LSTMs~\citep{hochreiter1997lstm} overcome vanishing gradients via gating but
process sequences unidirectionally.
Bidirectional variants and attention
mechanisms~\citep{vaswani2017attention} improve representation quality by
allowing direct connections between arbitrary positions.
The Temporal Fusion Transformer~\citep{lim2021temporal} combines variable
selection, recurrent encoding, and interpretable attention for multi-horizon
forecasting.

\textbf{Gap.}
Large Transformer architectures do not consistently outperform simpler baselines
on small benchmarks such as ETTh1~\citep{zeng2023ltsproblem}.
Hybrid residual decomposition~\citep{smyl2020hybrid} provides a middle ground:
statistical interpretation for the dominant component plus targeted deep
learning for residuals.

Covariates are four high-/medium-load usage channels (HUFL, HULL, MUFL, MULL).

\textbf{Cyclical time features (Phase 2).}
Hour-of-day and day-of-week are encoded as sine/cosine pairs so that
hour~23 and hour~0 are adjacent in feature space:
\[
  h^s_t = \sin\!\Bigl(\tfrac{2\pi\,\mathrm{h}(t)}{24}\Bigr),\quad
  h^c_t = \cos\!\Bigl(\tfrac{2\pi\,\mathrm{h}(t)}{24}\Bigr).
\]
Calendar features are always known and introduce no leakage.
Figure~\ref{fig:splits} visualises the split structure.

\section{Methodology}
% ===========================================================

\subsection{SARIMAX Linear Base}

We fit seasonal ARIMA order $(1,0,1)\times(1,0,1,24)$:
\[
  \Phi(B^{24})\,\phi(B)\,y_t = \Theta(B^{24})\,\theta(B)\,\varepsilon_t,
\]
where $B$ is the backshift operator.
No differencing ($d=0$) is applied because the Augmented Dickey--Fuller test
rejects non-stationarity.
Train residuals are $r_t = y_t - \hat{y}^{\mathrm{SARIMAX}}_t$.
For test evaluation the model is refit on train\,$\cup$\,val before
producing multi-step forecasts.

\subsection{GMM Regime Detection}

Residuals exhibit time-varying volatility.
A Gaussian Mixture Model with $K=2$ components is fit (on training data only)
to three \emph{causal} features computed from residuals:
lag-1 residual; 24\,h rolling standard deviation (using a past-only shift);
24\,h rolling mean of $|r|$.
The output is a soft regime vector $\mathbf{p}_t \in [0,1]^2$ for every
time point, fed as an auxiliary input to the network.

\subsubsection{BiLSTM Encoder}
A 2-layer bidirectional LSTM processes the 48-step encoder window.
Bidirectionality is valid here because the full past window is observed at
inference time (offline forecasting):
\[
  h_t = [\,\overrightarrow{h}_t\,;\,\overleftarrow{h}_t\,]\in\mathbb{R}^{2d},
  \quad d=64.
\]
Forget-gate biases are initialised to 1 to prevent early forgetting of
long-range context.

\subsubsection{Multi-Head Self-Attention}
BiLSTM outputs are projected to $d_{\mathrm{model}}=64$ and passed through
multi-head self-attention ($n_{\mathrm{heads}}=2$):
\[
  \mathrm{Attn}(Q,K,V)=\mathrm{softmax}\!\Bigl(\tfrac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\Bigr)V.
\]
Self-attention provides $\mathcal{O}(1)$ path length between any two time
steps, complementing the LSTM's local recurrence.

\subsubsection{Gated Residual Connection}
A gated residual wraps the attention block:
\[
  \mathbf{g} = \sigma(W_g\,h), \qquad
  \mathbf{z} = \mathrm{LN}\!\bigl(h + \mathbf{g}\odot\mathrm{ReLU}(W_r\,\mathrm{attn})\bigr).
\]
The gate suppresses noisy attention outputs early in training, preventing
gradient destabilisation while providing a direct gradient path.

\subsubsection{Decoder and Output}
Future regime probabilities and covariates are linearly projected and
mean-pooled to a context vector $\mathbf{c}$.
The fused representation is mapped to 24 residual forecasts:
$\hat{r}_{t+1:t+H}=W_2\,\mathrm{ReLU}(W_1[\mathbf{z};\mathbf{c}])$.

\subsection{Training and Regularisation}

\textbf{Optimiser.}
AdamW~\citep{loshchilov2019adamw} decouples weight decay from adaptive scaling,
restoring effective $\ell_2$ regularisation.

\textbf{Schedule.}
Linear warm-up over the first 10\,\% of steps, then cosine decay to zero:
\[
  \alpha(s)=\begin{cases}
    \alpha_{\max}\cdot s/s_w, & s < s_w,\\[2pt]
    \tfrac{\alpha_{\max}}{2}\bigl(1+\cos\pi\tfrac{s-s_w}{S-s_w}\bigr), & s\ge s_w.
  \end{cases}
\]

\textbf{Other.}
Dropout 0.2; gradient clipping (max norm 1.0); MSE on residuals;
early stopping (patience 10) restoring the best validation checkpoint.

\subsection{Hybrid Combination}

The final forecast is additive, not an ensemble average:
\[
  \hat{y}_{t+h} = \hat{y}^{\mathrm{SARIMAX}}_{t+h} + \hat{r}^{\mathrm{net}}_{t+h},
  \quad h=1,\ldots,24.
\]

\section{Experiments}
% ===========================================================

\subsection{Metrics}

\begin{align*}
  \mathrm{sMAPE} &= \tfrac{100}{n}\textstyle\sum\nolimits_i
    \tfrac{|y_i-\hat{y}_i|}{(|y_i|+|\hat{y}_i|)/2}, \\
  \mathrm{MAE}   &= \tfrac{1}{n}\textstyle\sum\nolimits_i|y_i-\hat{y}_i|, \\
  \mathrm{MASE}  &= \mathrm{MAE}\;/\;\overline{|y_t-y_{t-24}|}_{\mathrm{train}}.
\end{align*}

MASE uses the in-sample lag-24 naive error as scale; MASE\,$<1$ beats seasonal
naive.
We also evaluate an explicit \textbf{lag-24 seasonal naive} on the same
sliding test windows.

\section{Discussion}
% ===========================================================

\textbf{Why BiLSTM?}
The encoder window is fully observed at inference; bidirectional processing
is leakage-free and gives mid-window positions context from both directions.

\textbf{Why attention on top of LSTM?}
LSTMs have local recurrence bias; information from lag~24 or lag~48 must
traverse 24--48 gated steps.
Self-attention creates $\mathcal{O}(1)$ direct connections, capturing
same-hour-of-day patterns efficiently.

\textbf{Why a gated residual?}
A plain skip connection blindly adds attention output even when noisy early
in training.
The sigmoid gate suppresses the addition until attention becomes reliable,
preventing gradient destabilisation while maintaining a direct gradient path.

\textbf{Why AdamW over Adam?}
Adam's adaptive scaling distorts $\ell_2$ decay: features with large
gradients receive proportionally less regularisation.
AdamW applies weight decay independently of the gradient, restoring
well-calibrated regularisation~\citep{loshchilov2019adamw}.

\textbf{Limitations.}
SARIMAX orders are fixed (no automatic selection); decoder uses benchmark
covariates as known future inputs (valid for ETTh1, not real deployment);
only point forecasts are produced.

% ===========================================================
\section{Conclusion}
% ===========================================================

We present a Phase~2 hybrid temporal forecaster combining SARIMAX, GMM, and
TemporalHybridNet on ETTh1.
The main technical contributions are: bidirectional encoding with multi-head
self-attention and a gated residual; cyclical calendar features; AdamW with
warm-up and cosine schedule; and a systematic ablation study.
The strongest improvement over Phase~1 is diagnostic rigour---learning curves,
per-horizon analysis, and ablations show which components contribute.

The pipeline is fully reproducible:
\texttt{python scripts/run\_all.py} for the main run and
\texttt{python scripts/ablation.py} for ablations;
all figures and result CSVs are saved to \texttt{figures/}.
